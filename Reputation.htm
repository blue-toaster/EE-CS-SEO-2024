Reputation and Influence

1801 Portrait of Ludwig van Beethoven

Achievements

Beethoven's most significant accomplishment was elevating instrumental music, traditionally viewed as secondary to vocal music, to the pinnacle of artistic expression. In the 18th century, music was often considered inferior to literature and painting due to its nonimitative nature, with vocal forms like cantatas, operas, and oratorios being esteemed above instrumental compositions like sonatas and suites. Several factors contributed to a shift in perspective: the technical prowess of the Mannheim Orchestra, which facilitated the evolution of the symphony; a growing rejection of pure rationalism in favor of emotional depth among writers; and the influential works of Haydn and Mozart. However, it was Beethoven's groundbreaking contributions that ultimately inspired the late-Romantic notion, as articulated by Walter Pater, that "All arts aspire to the condition of music."


Following Beethoven, music could no longer be simply regarded as the creation of pleasing sounds. His instrumental pieces merged profound emotional depth with unparalleled structural sophistication, surpassing his predecessors in their mastery of musical forms, especially the symphony and the string quartet. Beethoven's legacy positioned him as a successor to Haydn rather than Mozart, whose notable achievements were primarily in opera and concertos.


Periods of work


First Period

Beethoven's early works, with the exception of a few pieces like the first two piano concertos, Creatures of Prometheus, and possibly the First Symphony (some interpretations include the Second Symphony as well), predominantly focus on chamber music, particularly compositions featuring the piano, Beethoven's instrument of choice. These pieces exhibit a meticulous attention to craftsmanship in the style of the 18th century. While bearing similarities to Haydn and Mozart in content, Beethoven's compositions of this period possess a slightly rougher and more direct quality typical of the era. Beethoven's approach to musical forms aligns more closely with Mozart than Haydn, characterized by expansive expositions with multiple themes and shorter developments. Slow movements are melodious and ornamented, while finales strike a balance between liveliness and sophistication.


Second Period

Distinctive elements that set Beethoven apart from his contemporaries include his innovative use of contrasting dynamics, notably crescendos leading to sudden soft passages, and his incorporation of improvisational techniques into his compositions, evident especially in his piano sonatas. The transition to Beethoven's second period is marked by works like the Opus 27 sonatas in 1801 and later by the Eroica Symphony (1804) and Fourth Piano Concerto (1806). In this phase, Beethoven further explores improvisatory elements within a traditional framework, delving into the logical implications of departing from established norms. His harmonic language remains straightforward compared to Mozart's, yet he manipulates it in relation to rhythm to create diverse stress patterns that shape each movement's structure uniquely.


The middle period sees Beethoven refining his use of improvisatory material, emphasizing logical development over adherence to classical conventions. His compositions exhibit shorter expositions, longer developments and codas, and a shift towards scherzos instead of minuets in the third movements. Finales gain prominence and complexity while decorative elements give way to more functional notes serving melodic and harmonic purposes. This period showcases Beethoven's distinct formal innovations and individuality in musical expression.


Third Period

In the third period, Beethoven's musical depth expands through intricate counterpoint influenced by Handel, evident in his adept use of fugues. This phase witnesses a broader harmonic palette and texture range alongside a deepening focus on musical complexity. Beethoven's exploration of folk themes adds richness to his compositions, offering a sense of redemption amidst evolving artistic intricacies. Variations become a significant focus in this period, showcasing Beethoven's mastery in creating sets with cumulative growth and profound emotional depth. His final string quartets and sonatas exemplify this growth through a blend of varied musical elements like fugues, dances, marches, and unique melodic structures.
